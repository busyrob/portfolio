Work

CarelonRx — Pharmacy Home (Sydney Member Portal)

Designing a pharmacy experience that adapts to the member

Pharmacy landing redesign → reduced clutter → task-first experience
Role: Design Advisor & Experience Lead
Context: Member-facing pharmacy experience
Focus: Pharmacy landing page, renewals, refills, search

The problem

The pharmacy landing page had become read-only and fragmented.

Members were forced to:

  • navigate page-to-page to take common actions
  • interpret dense, duplicative content
  • guess where to go for refills, renewals, or order status

While the business asked for “simplification,” what existed was already simple on the surface—but inefficient in practice.

The task

Our mandate was clear:

  • Simplify the pharmacy landing page
  • Streamline or remove duplicative functionality
  • Create intuitive entry points for common actions
  • Reduce the experience to the top reasons members come to Pharmacy

The assumption was that fewer options would automatically lead to better outcomes.

My opportunity

As we explored the problem, it became clear that removing functionality wasn’t the answer.

Members didn’t just need a simpler page—they needed a page that could adapt to them.

The real opportunity was to turn the pharmacy landing page into a working surface, not a signpost.

My role

I served as the design advisor and experience lead.

I was responsible for:

  • Challenging the assumption that simplification meant reduction
  • Guiding the experience toward progressive disclosure
  • Helping the team design a landing page that scaled with user need
  • Defending a counterintuitive approach with stakeholders
  • Supporting designers through critique, iteration, and testing

The design team executed the work. My role was to ensure clarity, confidence, and long-term viability of the solution.

Constraints

  • Regulated healthcare environment
  • Wide range of member familiarity and comfort with technology
  • Strong focus on older populations
  • Business pressure to “simplify” quickly

Designing a page that grows with the member

Instead of reducing the page to a static set of tiles or links, we designed the pharmacy home to expand based on relevance.

This approach was initially counterintuitive—but testing validated it decisively…

1. Turn the landing page into a tool
We enabled members to:

  • renew
  • refill
  • search for medications directly from the landing page.

This eliminated unnecessary navigation and guesswork.

2. Let content scale with need

  • Members with no prescriptions saw educational and marketing content
  • Members with active prescriptions saw actionable tools immediately
  • The more medications a member had, the more the page revealed

The experience grew—not cluttered.

3. Reduce guessing through clarity
Order status, pricing context, and actions were made visible and predictable.

Members no longer had to infer where information lived—the page made it obvious.

Testing & validation
While the approach initially raised concerns, testing revealed strong results—particularly well with older members—the group stakeholders were most concerned about.

The expandable, task-first model:

  • Reduced confusion
  • Increased confidence
  • Allowed members to act immediately without navigating away

This validation was critical in building trust around the direction.

Before & After

Before:
A read-only, fragmented landing page that pushed members elsewhere to act.

After:
A dynamic pharmacy home that supported action, learning, and growth in one place.

The goal wasn’t to make the page smaller—it was to make it smarter.

Outcome

The redesigned Pharmacy Home:

  • Reduced clutter without removing capability
  • Enabled common tasks directly on the landing page
  • Scaled naturally with member complexity
  • Performed particularly well with older users
  • Created a foundation that could evolve without redesigning from scratch

Why this mattered

Pulse is a daily touchpoint for associates. Improving clarity here had an outsized impact—not just on usability, but on how people felt starting their day.

This work demonstrated that focus and restraint can be just as transformative as new features.

What this changed

This project reinforced that simplicity is not about fewer options—it’s about fewer decisions.

My impact came from recognizing when a problem required restraint—and when it required conviction to push against an assumption that felt safe, but wasn’t correct.
