Future of Work — Desktop Concept

Anticipating the next default interaction model

Vision concept → leadership alignment → future-state exploration
Role: Design Advisor & Product Design Advisor
Context: Internal desktop application
Audience: Executive leadership
Timing: Demoed the same day Google/Gemini launched Disco

The context

Internal tools were evolving—but interaction patterns weren’t.

As AI capabilities accelerated, most enterprise products were still bound to:

  • page-based navigation
  • fragmented workflows
  • tool-by-tool mental models

The question wasn’t if work would change—it was what the next default interaction model would look like.

The opportunity

Rather than incrementally improving existing tools, this effort explored a future-state experience:

  • What if work wasn’t organized by pages?
  • What if context traveled with you?
  • What if information, action, and conversation lived side-by-side?

This was an opportunity to help leadership see what was coming.

My role

I served as a design and product advisor.

I was responsible for:

  • Framing the future-state interaction model
  • Guiding visual and experiential direction
  • Helping the team synthesize patterns emerging across AI tools
  • Shaping a narrative leadership could evaluate, not dismiss as speculative

The design team executed the visuals. My role was to ensure the concept felt credible, intentional, and grounded in real use.

Constraints

  • No production mandate—exploratory by design
  • Needed to feel relevant, not futuristic
  • Required confidence in direction, not exhaustive detail
  • High visibility with executive leadership

The design team executed the work. My role was to ensure clarity, confidence, and long-term viability of the solution.

Designing beyond pages

Rather than starting with features, we started with behavior.

The concept explored:

1. Persistent context
Work shouldn’t reset every time you navigate.

The experience kept:

  • conversation
  • history
  • intent

present across interactions.

2. Side-by-side modes
Instead of switching between tools, users could:

  • explore information
  • take action
  • reference context

without losing their place.

3. A calmer default state
The interface emphasized:

  • focus
  • spatial organization
  • reduced visual noise

The goal was clarity—not novelty.

Visual direction
Visually, the concept leaned into:

  • restraint over flourish
  • familiar patterns reassembled in new ways
  • confidence through simplicity

This helped the work feel inevitable, not experimental.

Timing & signal
The concept was shared with leadership on the same day Google/Gemini announced Disco.

This wasn’t coincidence—it was validation.

The work demonstrated that the organization was:

  • seeing the same signals
  • exploring the same interaction shifts
  • thinking at the right altitude

Outcome

The concept:

  • Resonated strongly with leadership
  • Helped align thinking around future interaction patterns
  • Positioned design as a strategic partner in shaping what comes next

More importantly, it reframed the conversation from features to ways of working.

What this represented

This project wasn’t about shipping a product.

It was about:

  • pattern recognition
  • confidence in direction
  • helping leadership evaluate the future with clarity instead of fear

Why this matters now

Design leadership isn’t only about solving today’s problems.

Sometimes, it’s about helping organizations see what’s already arriving—and preparing them to meet it with confidence.
