Pulse Redesign

Designing focus in a crowded system

Platform redesign → AI integration → improved engagement
Role: Design Advisor & Experience Lead
Context: Internal HR platform
Relationship: Informed by Associate Assist
Scope: Home/landing experience

The problem

Pulse had become a catch-all.

Over time, new features, announcements, and tools were layered onto the experience—each well-intentioned, but collectively overwhelming.

Associates struggled to:

  • find what mattered most
  • informed without feeling flooded
  • understand where to focus their attention

The experience lacked hierarchy.

The opportunity

The redesign wasn’t about adding more—it was about removing friction, clarifying priorities, and restoring focus.

Pulse needed to:

  • feel calm instead of crowded
  • highlight what mattered now
  • support both information and connection without competing for attention

This was also an opportunity to integrate Associate Assist in a way that felt natural—not bolted on.

My role

I served as the design advisor and experience lead.

I was responsible for:

  • Guiding experience simplification and hierarchy
  • Helping the team make difficult tradeoffs about what didn’t belong
  • Shaping how AI capabilities were introduced into an existing platform
  • Defending the work with stakeholders who had competing priorities

The design team executed the work. My role was to ensure clarity, cohesion, and confidence in the direction.

Constraints

  • Many stakeholders with competing needs
  • A platform already in heavy daily use
  • Strong attachment to existing features
  • Needed to introduce new capabilities without disrupting trust

Designing for focus

Rather than treating Pulse as a dashboard of everything, we reframed it as a place for orientation.

The experience was organized around three core needs:

1. What do I need to know?
News and updates were prioritized and editorialized—reducing noise while preserving relevance.

2. What do I need to do?
Key tools and actions were surfaced clearly, without competing for attention.

Associate Assist was introduced as a supportive layer—not the centerpiece—so it felt helpful rather than intrusive.

3. How do I stay connected?
Events and social moments were redesigned to feel more human and engaging, without overwhelming the page.

Experience principles

The redesign was guided by a few simple principles:

  • Clear hierarchy over equal weight
  • Less, but better
  • Calm first impressions
  • Familiar, not flashy

These principles helped the team navigate tough decisions when everything felt important.

Concept exploration

Outcome

The redesigned Pulse experience:

  • Reduced cognitive overload
  • Made key information easier to find
  • Created space for new capabilities without clutter
  • Felt calmer and more intentional for everyday use

It also established a framework for future updates—so the platform could evolve without becoming noisy again.

Why this mattered

Pulse is a daily touchpoint for associates. Improving clarity here had an outsized impact—not just on usability, but on how people felt starting their day.

This work demonstrated that focus and restraint can be just as transformative as new features.

Reflection

This project reinforced that design leadership is often about subtraction.

My impact came from helping the team and stakeholders agree on what mattered most—and having the conviction to protect that focus.
