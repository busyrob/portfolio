Work

Associate Assist

Centralizing enterprise knowledge through conversational AI

Concept → multi-system integration → internal adoption
Role: Design Advisor & Experience Lead
Context: Internal AI platform (Spark)
Scope: 13 internal tools

The problem

Associates needed information from dozens of internal tools—but accessing it required knowing where that information lived.

Even simple questions—like policy details, operational status, or process guidance—often required switching between systems, searching documentation, or asking around.

The result was friction, wasted time, and inconsistent answers.

The opportunity

Rather than improving individual tools in isolation, the opportunity was to remove the burden of navigation altogether.

If associates could simply ask a question and get a reliable answer, the system complexity could disappear behind the experience.

The goal wasn’t to replace tools—it was to orchestrate them.

My role

I served as the design advisor and experience lead.

I was responsible for:

  • Defining the experience vision for a conversational interface
  • Shaping how multiple systems would feel like a single product
  • Guiding designers through interaction patterns and tradeoffs
  • Helping align product, design, and leadership around a clear narrative

The design team executed the work. My role was to ensure the experience felt intentional, trustworthy, and scalable.

Constraints

  • 13 independent systems with different data models and behaviors
  • Early-stage internal AI platform
  • High expectations around accuracy and trust
  • Needed to feel useful immediately—not experimental

Designing a single place for answers

The core challenge wasn’t AI—it was experience coherence.

We focused on three principles:

1. You shouldn’t need to know where information lives
Associates shouldn’t have to understand internal system boundaries to get answers.

The experience needed to feel like:
“Ask once, get what you need.”

2. Answers must feel confident, not probabilistic
In an enterprise context, uncertainty erodes trust quickly.

We emphasized:

  • Clear sourcing
  • Direct language
  • Predictable response patterns

The goal was confidence over cleverness.

3. The system should reduce cognitive load, not add to it
Conversational AI can overwhelm users if it’s verbose or unfocused.

We prioritized:

  • Concise responses
  • Structured follow-ups
  • Clear next steps when more detail was needed
These principles guided how we framed the system and interaction model.
Concept exploration
Rather than positioning Associate Assist as “AI,” we framed it as a new way of working.
One place.
One interaction model.
One mental model.
This framing helped leadership and stakeholders see the value not as novelty—but as infrastructure.

Why this mattered

Associate Assist wasn’t just a product—it established a new interaction pattern for internal tools.

It demonstrated how AI could reduce friction without increasing cognitive load, and helped set expectations for how future internal experiences should feel.

Outcome

Associate Assist connected 13 of the most-used internal applications into a single conversational experience.

It:

  • Reduced friction for everyday questions
  • Eliminated the need to know where information lived
  • Established a foundation for future AI-driven experiences

The work also became a catalyst—later informing how AI capabilities were integrated into other internal platforms, including Pulse.

Reflection

This project reinforced that the hardest part of AI-enabled products isn’t intelligence—it’s trust and clarity.

My impact came from helping teams design an experience that felt simple, reliable, and human, despite the complexity underneath.
